About DELIVER.

DELIVER. is a rules-first enterprise project leadership simulation. The corrected build separates simulation logic from narrative text, removes direct frontend AI API calls, unlocks systems gradually, and keeps scoring deterministic.

Optional AI can be connected through a backend endpoint only. The AI should write narrative and coaching. The simulation engine should control consequences, score, failure, promotion, and memory.

How To Play DELIVER.

DELIVER. is an enterprise leadership simulation where every decision affects stakeholder trust, delivery performance, reputation, finances, burnout, and long-term career growth.


Gameplay Flow

1. Read The Situation

Analyze the business crisis, political tension, operational pressure, and project constraints presented in the scenario.

2. Review Organizational Signals

Check emails, stakeholders, RAID logs, EVM indicators, critical path threats, morale, and delivery health.

3. Make Strategic Decisions

Choose from predefined actions or write your own executive response. Every decision creates consequences across multiple systems.

4. Manage Consequences

Your choices influence trust, politics, delivery performance, burnout, reputation, governance, and future opportunities.

5. Progress Your Career

Rise from Associate Project Coordinator to Chief Delivery Officer while managing increasingly complex organizations and political environments.


Important Systems

  • Stakeholders: People remember your decisions and political behavior.
  • RAID: Risks, assumptions, issues, and dependencies must be controlled.
  • EVM: SPI and CPI track delivery and financial health.
  • Burnout: Stress affects leadership quality and team morale.
  • Reputation: Strong delivery increases executive trust and career growth.

Failure Condition

If Delivery Health and Stakeholder Confidence remain below 35% for two consecutive turns, the project collapses and the career fails.

Objective

The objective is not simply to complete projects. The real challenge is surviving organizational systems, political pressure, executive expectations, and strategic uncertainty.
